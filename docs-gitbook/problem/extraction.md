# The Extraction Problem

Every economic interaction you have generates value. Most of that value flows to intermediaries rather than to you.

## How Extraction Works

Consider a typical credit card purchase. When you buy something for $100:

- The merchant receives approximately $97
- Card networks (Visa, Mastercard) take $0.15-0.25
- The issuing bank takes $1.50-2.50
- Payment processors take $0.20-0.50

You paid $100. You received the product. You captured none of the economic surplus from the transaction itself.

But the extraction doesn't stop at transaction fees. Your purchase generates data: what you bought, where, when, how frequently you shop at that merchant, what time of day, whether you used a coupon. This data is packaged and sold to data brokers, who aggregate it into consumer profiles worth $50-200 per person annually.

## Categories of Extraction

**Transaction Fees**: Card interchange, payment processing, currency conversion. These fees are built into merchant prices, so you pay them indirectly on every purchase. Typical cost: 2-3% of all spending.

**Data Monetization**: Purchase histories, browsing behavior, location data, social graphs. Companies collect this data and sell it to brokers, advertisers, and AI training companies. You see none of this revenue. Estimated value: $200-500 per person annually.

**Attention Value**: When you scroll through social media or watch content with ads, your attention generates advertising revenue. Platforms capture this entirely. The average person generates $200-400 annually in attention value for platforms.

**Platform Surplus**: When you create content, leave reviews, or contribute to online communities, you're adding value to platforms. User-generated content drives engagement and increases platform value. Creators receive algorithmic exposure; platforms receive market cap appreciation.

## Scale Over a Lifetime

Conservative estimates for a typical consumer over 40 years of economic activity:

| Category | Annual Extraction | 40-Year Total |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Transaction fees | $1,500 | $60,000 |
| Data monetization | $350 | $14,000 |
| Attention value | $300 | $12,000 |
| **Total** | **$2,150** | **$86,000** |

This doesn't account for compound growth. If you had captured and invested even half of this extraction at 7% annual returns, you'd have substantially more.

## The Infrastructure Gap

Why don't individuals capture this value? Because the infrastructure doesn't exist.

Card networks have global real-time transaction processing. Data brokers have sophisticated aggregation and matching systems. Advertising platforms have behavioral prediction models refined over decades.

What infrastructure exists for individuals to intercept their share of these flows? Essentially none.

You can't opt out of interchange fees. You can't easily license your own data. You can't sell your attention directly. The systems were built for institutional capture, and individual participation was never part of the design.

## Why This Matters Now

The extraction problem has existed for decades, but it's about to get worse.

As AI agents begin mediating economic activity, the volume of transactions and data generated will increase dramatically. Agents will make purchases, negotiate deals, access services, and manage finances. Every interaction will generate extractable value.

If agents plug into existing infrastructure, extraction accelerates. Banks and platforms will capture more value more efficiently through agent-mediated transactions.

But if new infrastructure is built with individual capture in mind, agents could redirect these flows to users instead.

That's the window we're in now: the infrastructure decisions made in the next few years will determine who captures value in the agent economy.
